The ring is on your finger, the celebratory champagne is gone, and your social media notifications have finally slowed down. You might feel a frantic urge to start scrolling through venue websites or pinning decor ideas immediately. But if you jump into booking vendors before taking these five steps, you will likely spend more money than you intended and create unnecessary stress for yourself later.

Before you touch a wedding planning app or set a single date, complete these five tasks to build a solid foundation.

1. Create a “Hard Limit” Budget

Most couples make the mistake of setting a “goal” budget rather than a “hard limit” budget. A goal budget is a number you wish to spend; a hard limit is the absolute ceiling you cannot exceed without going into debt or sacrificing other financial goals.

Sit down with your partner and look at your actual bank statements. Identify three buckets:

  • Personal Savings: What is available right now?
  • Monthly Contributions: How much can you save between today and the wedding date?
  • Contributions from Family: If family members have offered to pay, get a specific dollar amount. Do not rely on vague promises like, “we’ll take care of the bar.” Ask for a number, or ask them to pay a specific vendor directly.

Once you have that total, subtract 10% immediately. That 10% is your “emergency buffer” for taxes, service charges, and last-minute price hikes. The remaining 90% is your working budget.

2. Define the “Non-Negotiables”

You and your partner likely have very different visions of what makes a wedding “good.” You might care deeply about a live seven-piece band, while your partner might be more concerned with having a high-end, open-bar cocktail hour.

Sit down separately and write down your top three priorities. Then, compare lists. If you agree on everything, great. If you don’t—for example, you want a huge guest list and your partner wants an intimate destination wedding—you have to resolve this before you pick a venue. If you book a 200-person venue, you have lost the ability to have an intimate wedding. Prioritizing now prevents the “we’re paying for things we don’t even care about” regret later.

3. Draft the “Raw” Guest List

Do not estimate your guest count based on what sounds reasonable. Open a spreadsheet and list every single person you might want to invite. Include parents’ friends, coworkers, and distant relatives.

For many couples, this initial list will hit 200 or 250 names. If your budget is $20,000, hosting 200 people leaves you with $100 per guest to cover food, drink, rentals, service, and venue fees. In most of the U.S., that is nearly impossible to pull off without compromising on quality or venue aesthetics.

Seeing the raw number helps you decide if you need to trim the list or increase the budget. It is much easier to have the “who makes the cut” conversation now than it is after you have already toured venues that are too small for your actual list.

4. Determine Your Ideal “Planning Style”

Are you two people who like to manage projects, or do you find administrative work draining? This dictates whether you need a full-service planner, a month-of coordinator, or if you can handle the logistics yourself.

If you both work 50-hour weeks, trying to plan a wedding from scratch will feel like a second job. If you love spreadsheets and DIY projects, you might save thousands by managing the details yourselves. Be honest about your capacity. If you know you are prone to procrastination, lean toward hiring a coordinator early so they can keep you on track. Booking a coordinator after you have already made mistakes with contracts doesn’t help as much as having them there to review the first one.

5. Discuss the “Date Strategy”

Instead of picking one specific day, choose a “date window.” For example, aim for “any Friday or Sunday in September or October.”

The wedding industry operates on supply and demand. A Saturday in June is at a premium price point, whereas a Friday in November might come with significant discounts or lower food and beverage minimums at the exact same venue. By having a flexible date window, you give yourself the power to shop around. If you are dead-set on one specific Saturday, you lose all leverage when negotiating with venues and vendors, because you are locked into their pricing for that high-demand slot.

Once you have addressed these five areas, you will find that the actual planning process—the timelines, the color palettes, and the vendor meetings—becomes much less overwhelming. You won’t be guessing; you will be executing a plan that you have already vetted for feasibility and alignment with your shared goals.

With these foundational tasks complete, you are ready to start building your actual event timeline. The next step is to research venues that fit both your budget and your guest count, armed with the realistic numbers you just calculated.
