Planning a wedding in your hometown is a massive undertaking, but planning one across oceans, borders, and time zones is an entirely different beast. When my partner and I decided to tie the knot on a cliffside in Italy while living in Chicago, we quickly realized that standard wedding checklists just would not cut it. Between coordinating video calls across a six-hour time difference and trying to figure out foreign postal services, we had to invent our own system.

If you are staring down the prospect of a destination wedding, do not panic. Breaking the process down into a chronological roadmap will keep your stress levels low and your logistics tight. Here is the exact month-by-month timeline I wish I had when I started planning.

12 to 14 Months Out: Location, Budget, and the Non-Negotiables

Before you look at a single flight price, you need to establish three things: your total budget, your estimated guest count, and your priority list. Destination wedding costs hide a lot of sneaky line items, from international wire transfer fees to local government filing taxes.

Start by researching legal requirements for marriage in your chosen country. Some nations require you to be in the country for a specific number of days before the ceremony, while others require extensive paperwork that must be translated and notarized by your local consulate.

  • Establish a shared digital calendar: Pick a cloud-based calendar that automatically displays your time zone alongside your venue’s local time so you never miss a video consultation.
  • Decide on a site visit: If your budget allows, plan a scouting trip 10 to 12 months out to taste the food, walk the grounds, and meet your primary vendors face-to-face.
  • Draft the guest count early: A local resort in the Caribbean has very different capacity limits than a historic villa in Tuscany.

9 to 11 Months Out: Securing the Venue and Key Vendors

Your venue and your wedding planner are your foundation. If you are getting married in a remote area or a popular tourist destination, top-tier venues book out over a year in advance.

If your venue does not include an in-house coordinator, hiring a local wedding planner who speaks the native language and understands local customs is non-negotiable. They will be your boots on the ground when you are thousands of miles away. Once your venue is locked in, secure your photographer, videographer, and caterer.

Do not wait to send your save-the-dates for a destination wedding. Guests need at least nine months of lead time to request time off work, budget for travel, and secure flights, especially if your wedding falls during peak tourist season.

6 to 8 Months Out: Website, Travel Blocks, and Attire

By this point, your save-the-dates should be in the mail, directing guests to a comprehensive wedding website. Your website needs to do heavy lifting: include flight tips, airport transfer options, dress code suggestions, and hotel room block information.

  • Set up room blocks: Negotiate a block of rooms at a nearby hotel or resort, and provide alternative lodging options at different price points for your guests.
  • Shop for attire: Keep the climate in mind. Heavy silks or structured ballgowns can turn a tropical beach ceremony into an endurance test.
  • Establish a communication rhythm: Set up bi-weekly or monthly video check-ins with your local planner to review progress and keep tasks manageable.

4 to 5 Months Out: Paperwork, Invitations, and Logistics

Now is the time to tackle the administrative hurdles. Submit your official invitations—mailed or digital—giving guests an RSVP deadline at least three months before the wedding so you can finalize numbers for the caterer.

If you are dealing with international paperwork, begin submitting your birth certificates, certificates of no impediment, and passport copies to the appropriate embassies or local municipalities. Keep physical copies of every single document in a dedicated folder that travels with you in your carry-on luggage.

2 to 3 Months Out: Details, Design, and Timeline

With the big pieces in place, focus on the details that make the wedding feel personal. Finalize your menu tastings, floral designs, and rentals.

If you are bringing decor items from home—such as custom favors, welcome bag items, or signage—calculate how much space they will take up in your luggage. I learned the hard way that trying to pack heavy acrylic welcome signs in checked luggage is risky. Instead, see if you can source items locally or design digital signage that the venue can print on-site.

Draft a minute-by-minute wedding day timeline and send it to all your vendors in both your home time zone and the local time zone to avoid any confusion about setup windows.

1 Month Out: Final Payments and Packing

The final weeks are all about tying up loose ends. Send final headcounts to your venue and caterer, confirm arrival times for all vendors, and pay any remaining balances.

Pack your wedding attire, shoes, rings, and legal documents in your carry-on bags. Never check anything you cannot live without for the wedding day. Ship out any non-perishable welcome bag items ahead of time if your resort accepts packages, and confirm delivery with the concierge.

Managing a destination wedding takes patience, flexibility, and a willingness to surrender a bit of control to your local team. As you start mapping out your own timeline and weighing your vendor options, consider the tools and support systems available to keep your planning process organized from afar.
