When I started planning my own wedding, the bar package was the single most intimidating line item in my budget. Looking at the “per-head” pricing—often ranging from $40 to $80 per person—felt like writing a blank check to a caterer. That is when I realized that many venues are flexible if you know how to propose a BYOB arrangement that limits their liability while saving you a fortune.

If you are looking to cut costs, negotiating a corkage fee structure is one of the most effective levers you can pull. Here is how you can approach it without getting laughed out of the venue coordinator’s office.

Why Venues Hate BYOB (And How to Address It)

Before you send that first email, understand the venue’s perspective. They are not just charging for the liquid; they are charging for the liquor license, the insurance, the liability, and the staff to pour it. When they say “no outside alcohol,” they aren’t just being greedy—they are protecting themselves from a lawsuit if a guest gets intoxicated and leaves the property.

To get a “yes,” your pitch needs to prioritize their safety and profit. Do not just ask to bring your own beer; offer a solution that keeps their staff involved and their liability managed.

The “Hybrid” Negotiating Strategy

The most successful negotiation I’ve seen involves a hybrid model. Instead of asking for a total BYOB wedding, which many venues will immediately reject, propose a “partial” setup.

For example, suggest that the venue provides the bar staff and the mixers, but you supply the spirits, wine, and beer. By keeping their bartenders on the clock, you ensure the venue is still in control of how much guests are served. This makes the venue feel much more comfortable because a professional staff member is still monitoring consumption.

When you present this to the venue manager, use this language: “I am happy to pay a flat per-bottle corkage fee for the wine and a per-person service fee for the bartender’s time, provided we can source the alcohol ourselves. This keeps your staff in charge of service and ensures all liability protocols are followed.”

How to Calculate the Corkage Math

Before you commit, run the numbers on a spreadsheet. A venue might charge you $15 per bottle of wine as a corkage fee. If you purchase high-quality wine at wholesale or bulk pricing for $10 a bottle, your total cost is $25. Compare that to a venue’s “house red” which they might charge you $40 or $50 for.

Here is the breakdown of what to look for when evaluating an offer:

  • The Flat Fee: This is your best-case scenario. You pay a set amount (e.g., $500 total) for the privilege of bringing your own items, regardless of volume.
  • The Per-Bottle Fee: This is standard. Expect $10–$20 per bottle for wine and $2–$5 per bottle for beer.
  • The “Pour Fee”: If they won’t do corkage, ask for a service-only fee. This is where you pay for the staff to open and pour, but you don’t pay a markup on the product.

Always negotiate a “return” clause in your contract. If you bring 20 cases of wine and only use 15, ensure the contract states that the unopened bottles remain yours. You don’t want to pay a corkage fee on alcohol that never even left the box.

The Logistics of Delivery and Storage

The biggest hurdle for the venue is space. They don’t want your crates of beer cluttering their storage room for three days. To make your request more appealing, take the logistics off their plate:

  • Coordinate the drop-off: Arrange to have the alcohol delivered by your supplier on the morning of the wedding, or drop it off yourself during the final walkthrough.
  • Provide an inventory list: Give the venue manager a printed sheet detailing exactly how many bottles of each type are being delivered. This prevents “lost” inventory and keeps the staff organized.
  • Choose easy-pour options: If you are bringing your own beer, avoid kegs unless the venue specifically has a draft system. Kegs are messy and require specific equipment. Stick to cans or bottles. They are easier for the bartenders to chill and distribute, which makes them much more likely to approve your request.

When to Walk Away

If a venue insists on a mandatory, high-priced bar package and refuses any conversation about corkage or service fees, you have to weigh that against the rest of your budget. If the bar package adds $10,000 to your bottom line, that is money that could have gone toward your photographer, your band, or your honeymoon.

Sometimes, the “all-inclusive” venues are actually cheaper in the long run because they have volume discounts you can’t get on your own. Always calculate the “total cost of service” before deciding if the BYOB path is actually the cheaper route.

By approaching the venue with a professional plan that prioritizes their liability concerns, you demonstrate that you are a serious client. Most managers would rather work with a couple who has a plan than a couple who is just trying to cut corners without considering the logistics.

As you begin reaching out to potential venues, keep a copy of your guest list and a rough estimate of your alcohol consumption ready. Having these numbers in your back pocket will make it much easier to move into the final stages of your contract negotiation.
